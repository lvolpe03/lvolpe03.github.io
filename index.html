Project Goals

Human migration is one of the defining global trends of the 21st century, whether it is political, economic, or conflit-driven. The goal of this project is to investigate patterns in the movement of Ukrainian refugees as a result of the February 2022 Russian invasion. I will also be analyzing the relationships between refugee flows and host country indicators, with the final goal of identifying the political and economic indicators that are most predictive of having larger refugee populations.

The Ukrainian refugee crisis is the fastest growing and largest displacement crisis in Europe since WWII (UNHCR). Political institutional quality and economic strength are big determinants of integration success, and knowing which indicators are most related to refugee flows can help governments and aid agencies help refugees more effectively.

Statistics for Time-Series Analysis

Another important aspect of my project is its temporal dimension; the refugee data I have collected is a 3-year long panel of different countries. While the number of periods in the panel is low, there are still important statistics we can generate that will help our later modeling. For example, lags of refugee counts are essential in autoregressive modeling if I want to account for the serial correlation between observations. I can also compute discrete growth rates and log-differences of a country's refugee population from year to year, revealing how volatile or stable the flows are over time.

In[]:
# Lags for the variable Ref_Asylum
df_RefWDI['Ref_Asylum_t-1'] = df_RefWDI.groupby('Country of Asylum')['Ref_Asylum'].shift(1)
df_RefWDI['Ref_Asylum_t-2'] = df_RefWDI.groupby('Country of Asylum')['Ref_Asylum'].shift(2)

# Replace NaNs in lagged refugee counts with zeros because pre-war, they had zero refugees.
df_RefWDI.fillna({'Ref_Asylum_t-1': 0}, inplace = True)
df_RefWDI.fillna({'Ref_Asylum_t-2': 0}, inplace = True)

# Now log transform these lags as well to make them compatible with our dependent variable.
df_RefWDI['ln_Ref_Asylum_t-1'] = np.log(df_RefWDI['Ref_Asylum_t-1'] + 1)
df_RefWDI['ln_Ref_Asylum_t-2'] = np.log(df_RefWDI['Ref_Asylum_t-2'] + 1)

# Also compute log-differences for Ref_Asylum
df_RefWDI['Ref_Growth_LD'] = df_RefWDI['ln_Ref_Asylum'] - df_RefWDI['ln_Ref_Asylum_t-1']
# Discrete growth rates
df_RefWDI['Ref_Growth'] = (df_RefWDI['Ref_Asylum'] - df_RefWDI['Ref_Asylum_t-1']) / df_RefWDI['Ref_Asylum_t-1'] * 100
In[]:
display(df_RefWDI.sort_values(by='Ref_Growth_LD', ascending=False).head(5))
display(df_RefWDI[df_RefWDI['Ref_Growth'] != np.inf].sort_values(by='Ref_Growth', ascending=False).head(5))

As we can see from the above tables, in the log-differences version, the single largest growth episodes were in the first year of the war to countries like Russia, Germany, and Poland, and at a massive scale; Russia's log-difference of 14.1 indicates growth in refugee population by a factor of about 1.2 million. However, this is expected because log-difference growth starting from zero would be very large compared to growth between years with non-zero refugee counts. Furthermore, in such an explosive conflict, it makes sense contextually that the first year would see the largest outflow of Ukrainian refugees.

Looking at discrete growth, since the 2022 rates start from zero, those observed rates are mathematically infinite. Excluding those occurrences to look at growth after the first year of arrivals in a given country, we can see that Brazil had the next largest relative increase in refugee population, up 973% from 108 to 1159 in 2023. In fact, we can see that all of the top 5 single largest increases (outside 2022) occurred in 2023, so we know that the rate of refugee flows appears to have started high and slowly decreased from year to year in this data set.

After mean imputation, we still have a decent number of NaNs in some of our variables. This is because some of the countries have missing values for all three years, so there is no data to average in the first imputation method. There are also some countries that only appear in the dataset once (i.e. they only recorded Ukrainian refugees in one year) and are missing an indicator value, so they cannot be averaged either. To fill in these values, we can use SciKit Learn to run a K-nearest neighbors imputation on the remaining NaNs.

Modeling

Now we are ready for the final phase of the project: modeling. This OLS regression model will be help tell us which political and economic indicators in a country may make them more likely to take more refugees in times of crisis.

OLS Regression

One important objective in OLS regressions is avoiding multicollinearity between regressors. If two or more variables are too highly correlated (meaning they would essentially measure the same relationship with the outcome variable) or have direct linear dependency, the model will struggle to estimate the unique effect of each variable. This would also lead to unstable coefficients and inflated standard errors, making proper statistical inference impossible.

I choose to modify two existing variables to avoid this. Below, we can see the coefficients between the measured government rankings are quite high, so I combine these into a composite rank index with equal weighting. Like with my dependent variable, I also log transform GNI per capita because it follows a similarly right-skewed distribution, and transforming it will enable me to find a more precise pattern from this variable.

In[]:
# Calculate correlation between 'RuleLaw_rank', 'GovEffect_rank', and 'Pol_Stab_rank'
display(df_final[['RuleLaw_rank', 'GovEffect_rank', 'Pol_Stab_rank']].corr())
# Build composite index out of governance ranks due to correlation
df_final['Governance_index'] = (df_final['RuleLaw_rank'] + df_final['GovEffect_rank'] + df_final['Pol_Stab_rank']) / 3

# Log transform GNI_pc to fix skew; rich-country income differences are small relative to global range
df_final['ln_GNI_pc'] = np.log(df_final['GNI_pc'])

Here, we run our final model. When I first ran this model with each regressor in level terms (except ln_GNI_pc and the lags, which are already transformed by definition), because the variables were in such vastly different unit spaces, the condition number was too high for proper inference. However, once I z-standardized the regressors, these estimates stabilized.

The only regressors that were not scaled are the two lags of refugee count and ln_GNI_pc to preserve comparability with the DV, as well as clean interpretability as elasticities.

Results and Interpretation

In the regression table, we should only look at the statistically significant coefficients, meaning they have p-values less than or equal to 0.05. From our results, besides the two lag values that are obviously highly explanatory, the most significant predictive indicators are Edu_UpSec25p, ln_GNI_pc, Pop_Growth, and FoodInsec_pct. Significant at the 10% threshold is also Pop_15_64_pct.

For the scaled regressors, coefficients must be exponentiated and are interpreted in terms of standard deviations. For ln_GNI_pc, this result is interpreted as an elasticity because both it and the outcome variable are log-transformed.

For example, a one-SD increase in the share of the host population with educational attainment of at least upper secondary is associated with a 0.969 increase in log refugee popoulation, on average.

  • e^0.969 - 1 = 163% increase in refugees

Second example: A one-SD increase in the share of the population suffering from food insecurity is associated with a -0.785 decrease in logged refugees, on average.

  • e^-0.785 - 1 = 54.4% decrease in refugee population

Additionally, on average, a 1% increase in GNI per capita is associated with a 0.98% increase in refugee population: a near proportionate elasticity. This can be scaled up linearly to predict flows based on however much a host country may have increased their GNI per capita by in a given year, holding other factors constant.

Conclusion

Based on the positivity and negativity of certain coefficients in our regressions, we can identify various "push" and "pull" factors in the context of refugee movement. Positive coefficients on Edu_UpSec25p and ln_GNI_pc indicate that refugees cluster in wealthier, higher-educated, and institutionally capable socities. Negative coefficients on Pop_Growth and FoodInsec_pct indicate that refugees tend to not go to societies with existing internal socioeconomic stress. The hope is that insights like these can help governments and organizations be better prepared for future migrant crises.
